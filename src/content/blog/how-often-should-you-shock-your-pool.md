For most residential pools in swim season, **shock every 1 to 2 weeks** as a baseline — and shock immediately when specific events happen. But the calendar is a backup plan. The real trigger is your **combined chlorine** reading. Here's how to think about it.

## The baseline schedule

A regular shock keeps chlorine ahead of the contaminants that build up between doses. A reasonable default for a home pool in active use:

- **Weekly** in hot weather with heavy use.
- **Every two weeks** for lighter use or cooler weather.
- **Less often** for a pool that's mostly idle — but never skip testing.

Think of this as insurance, not the whole strategy. Two identical pools can need very different schedules depending on bather load, sunlight, and rain.

## Shock now, regardless of schedule, when:

- **After heavy use** — a pool party puts in a lot of sweat, sunscreen, and organic load.
- **After a rainstorm** — rain dilutes chlorine and drops contaminants and phosphates into the water.
- **After a contamination event** — a pet in the pool, or any fecal/vomit incident (follow public-health hyperchlorination guidance for these).
- **When you smell "chlorine."** That strong pool smell is *chloramines* (combined chlorine), not free chlorine — a sign you're overdue.
- **When the water looks dull, hazy, or slightly green.**
- **At opening and closing** — see the season-startup approach on the [pool opening calculator](/pool-opening/).

## The real signal: combined chlorine

The most reliable trigger isn't a date — it's a test. Measure **total chlorine** and **free chlorine**; the difference is **combined chlorine (chloramines)**:

> Combined chlorine = Total chlorine − Free chlorine

When combined chlorine climbs above about **0.5 ppm**, it's time to shock — you need to reach *breakpoint* to burn those chloramines off. That's the same breakpoint math the [shock calculator](/) uses, so you add enough to actually clear them rather than half-dosing and leaving the smell behind.

## Does shock type change the frequency?

Not the *frequency* so much as the *handling*:

- **Cal-hypo** is a strong, common choice for scheduled shocking — [the cal-hypo calculator](/cal-hypo/) gives you pounds, ounces, and cups. Watch calcium buildup over a season.
- **Liquid chlorine** dissolves instantly and adds no CYA or calcium, nice for frequent dosing.
- **Non-chlorine shock** can be used between chlorine shocks for oxidation with a short swim wait, but it won't handle algae.

## Don't over-shock

Shocking more often than you need wastes chemicals, drives up CYA or calcium depending on product, and means [longer waits before swimming](/blog/how-long-after-shocking-can-you-swim/). If you're tempted to shock constantly, the fix is usually better weekly chemistry and correct dosing — not more shock. And if you've already gone too high, here's [how to bring chlorine back down safely](/blog/can-you-over-shock-a-pool/).

## Bottom line

Shock every 1–2 weeks as a floor, shock on triggers like storms and parties, and let your combined-chlorine reading make the final call. When it's time, dose accurately with [the calculator](/) so one treatment does the job.
