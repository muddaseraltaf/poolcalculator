You shocked the pool, waited, and it's still green — or green-turned-cloudy. This is one of the most common pool problems, and it almost always comes down to one of seven causes. Here's how to diagnose which one you're facing and fix it.

## First, read the color — it tells you the cause

Before you add anything else, look closely at *what kind* of green you have. The shade and clarity narrow the problem down fast:

| What you see | Most likely cause | Where to look |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Bright/deep green, cloudy, slimy walls | Live algae, under-dosed | Causes 1, 2, 3 |
| Clear but tinted green, no slime | Dissolved metals, not algae | Cause 4 |
| Dull gray-green or teal haze | Dead algae in suspension | Causes 5, 6 |
| Clears, then re-greens in days | Regrowth from phosphates/low residual | Cause 7 |

If the walls feel slippery and the water is cloudy, it's almost certainly living algae and you're in causes 1–3. If the water is *clear* but colored, skip to cause 4 before you waste another bag of shock.

## 1. You didn't add enough shock

This is the number one reason. Clearing an algae bloom is not a maintenance dose — it takes **breakpoint chlorination**, which can mean raising free chlorine to 3–4x a normal shock, held there until the green is gone. A single bag in a big pool won't touch a serious bloom.

The trap most people fall into: they add "a bag or two," see a little improvement, then stop. Free chlorine dips back below the threshold overnight and the algae recovers. Breakpoint only works if you reach it *and hold it* — free chlorine needs to stay elevated continuously until the water clears, which for a heavy bloom can mean re-dosing over two or three days.

**Fix:** dose for the actual severity and keep free chlorine up until it's clear. [The green pool calculator](/green-pool/) is preset to algae-clearing targets and scales to your pool volume, so you add enough the first time instead of chasing it with half-doses. Re-test a few hours after dosing — if FC has crashed back down, that's the algae eating it, and you add more.

## 2. Your CYA (stabilizer) is too high

Cyanuric acid protects chlorine from the sun, but too much of it handcuffs your chlorine — the free chlorine reads fine on a strip but can't actually oxidize algae effectively. Above ~50 ppm CYA, you need proportionally more chlorine; above ~100 ppm, shock becomes an uphill battle.

This is the single most under-diagnosed cause. People whose pools *won't clear no matter how much they shock* almost always have runaway CYA, usually from months of trichlor tablets (every puck adds stabilizer). The chlorine is there on paper — it just can't do its job. A rough rule the pool-chemistry community uses: your shock target scales with CYA, so a pool at 100 ppm CYA needs roughly double the free chlorine of a pool at 40 ppm to hit the same killing power.

**Fix:** test CYA. If it's very high, the only real cure is a partial drain and refill to dilute it — there's no chemical that removes CYA reliably. Replace a third to half the water, re-test, then re-shock. Going forward, shock with an unstabilized product like [cal-hypo](/cal-hypo/) or [liquid chlorine](/liquid-chlorine/) so you're not adding more CYA every time.

## 3. pH is out of range

Chlorine's killing power drops sharply as pH rises. At pH 8.0, a large fraction of your chlorine is in the weak, ineffective form (hypochlorite ion) rather than the strong form (hypochlorous acid) that actually kills algae. You can dump in shock and get little effect.

Here's the catch that trips people up: **adding cal-hypo shock itself pushes pH up.** So if your pH was already high, shocking can nudge it higher and further blunt the very chlorine you just added. That's why the order matters.

**Fix:** bring pH down to 7.2–7.4 *before* shocking. This one change often makes the difference between shock that works and shock that seems to do nothing. If you're using cal-hypo, test pH again after the bloom clears and bring it back into range.

## 4. It's not algae — it's metals

If the water turned green (sometimes right after you added chlorine) but there's no slime and the water is fairly clear, you may have dissolved **copper or iron**, not algae. Chlorine oxidizes the metal and stains the water green or brown. More shock makes it worse.

Tell-tale signs: the green appeared suddenly right after shocking rather than building over days; the water is transparent rather than cloudy; you may see staining on light-colored surfaces or a green tint to blonde hair. Common sources are well water, corroded copper plumbing or a heater, and copper-based algaecides.

**Fix:** test for metals. Use a metal sequestrant to bind them, run the filter, and address the source — switch off copper algaecides and consider a hose pre-filter if your fill water is the culprit. Do *not* keep shocking; you'll just oxidize more metal and deepen the stain.

## 5. The algae is dead — you're seeing cloudy debris

Good news disguised as bad: if the pool shifted from clear-green to a dull, cloudy teal or gray, the algae is likely dead and suspended in the water. Shock did its job; now it's a filtration problem, not a chemistry one.

The quick confirmation: test free chlorine. If it's holding high and steady (not getting eaten), the algae is dead and you just need to clear the corpses out of the water. If FC keeps crashing, the algae is still alive — back to causes 1–3.

**Fix:** run the filter continuously, brush, and add a clarifier (gathers fine particles so the filter catches them) or a flocculant (drops everything to the floor to vacuum to waste). Clean or backwash the filter as it loads up — with a heavy kill it may clog several times before the water polishes clear.

## 6. Your filter and circulation can't keep up

Dead or dying algae has to physically leave the water. A dirty filter, short run times, or poor circulation leaves it hanging there looking green.

A filter that's been fighting an algae bloom gets dirty fast, and a clogged filter barely moves water. Sand and DE filters need backwashing (or a DE recharge) once pressure climbs; cartridge filters need hosing off or soaking. Undersized run times are the other half — clearing a bloom is not the time for a 4-hour daily schedule.

**Fix:** backwash or clean the filter, run the pump 24/7 until clear, and brush walls and floor daily to keep debris moving to the filter. Check filter pressure and clean it again whenever it rises about 8–10 psi above its clean baseline.

## 7. Phosphates are feeding regrowth

High phosphate levels are algae food. If your pool clears then greens again within days, phosphates (from fertilizer runoff, some fill water, leaves and debris, or certain products) may be fueling a fast comeback.

Phosphates aren't the *cause* of the original bloom — chlorine that's too low or too weak is — but they let algae rebound quickly the moment your free-chlorine residual dips. If you've fixed chemistry and filtration but keep re-greening, this is the usual suspect.

**Fix:** test and treat with a phosphate remover, then maintain a steady free-chlorine residual. Keep leaves and lawn fertilizer out of the pool, and don't rely on the phosphate remover alone — it's a supplement to good chlorination, not a substitute.

## The efficient order of operations

1. Test and correct **pH** (7.2–7.4) and check **CYA**.
2. Brush the whole pool to break up algae.
3. Shock to **breakpoint** — use [the green pool calculator](/green-pool/) for the right amount.
4. Run the filter continuously and keep brushing.
5. Re-test; re-shock if free chlorine won't hold.
6. Once clear, clarify to polish, then get back on a regular schedule.

## How long should it take to clear a green pool?

With correct dosing and the filter running around the clock, a mild green tint often clears in 24 hours, and a moderate bloom in 2–3 days. A deep, dark-green "swamp" can take 4–5 days and several re-doses as the algae dies off in stages. If nothing has changed after 24 hours of properly high free chlorine, stop and re-check CYA and pH — one of them is almost certainly blocking the chlorine.

## Should I use algaecide instead of more shock?

Chlorine does the heavy lifting; algaecide is a follow-up, not a first response. Shock to breakpoint to kill the bloom, then use algaecide afterward as prevention once the water is clear. Avoid copper-based algaecides if you already have metal concerns (see cause 4). Never mix algaecide directly with a fresh, heavy shock dose — add them at different times.
